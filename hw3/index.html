Overview

In this homework I built the core of a path tracer. I implemented camera ray generation, pixel sampling, and ray intersection for triangles and spheres, then added BVH acceleration to make rendering practical for larger scenes. After that I implemented direct lighting, indirect lighting with recursive bounces and Russian Roulette, and adaptive sampling. One thing I liked in the earlier part was that debug shading based on ray direction was enough to check whether ray generation was working before doing the full lighting pipeline.

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

For camera ray generation, I mapped the normalized image coordinates to the sensor plane at z = -1 in camera space. The sensor goes from -tan(hFov/2) to tan(hFov/2) horizontally and same for vertical with vFov. I lerped using x and y to get the point on the sensor, then built a ray from the origin through that point. I normalized the direction, rotated it into world space using c2w, and set the origin to the camera position in world space. min_t and max_t are set to nclip and fclip.

For raytrace_pixel, I took ns_aa random samples with gridSampler. For each sample I added the offset to the pixel position, divided by image width and height to normalize, then called generate_ray and est_radiance_global_illumination. I averaged all the results and wrote to the sample buffer.

For triangle intersection I used the Möller-Trumbore algorithm. It solves for t, b1, b2 by setting up the ray and barycentric form as a linear system. If b1 ≥ 0, b2 ≥ 0, b1 + b2 ≤ 1, and t is in [min_t, max_t], the hit is valid. I updated max_t and used b1, b2 to interpolate the vertex normals for the surface normal.

For sphere intersection I plugged the ray into the sphere equation and got a quadratic. If discriminant is negative there is no hit. Otherwise I try the smaller t first and if it's not in range I try the larger one. The normal is the vector from sphere center to the hit point, normalized.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

For BVH construction, I first computed a bounding box over all primitives in the current range. If the count was at or below max_leaf_size, I made it a leaf node and set start and end.

Otherwise, I computed the bounding box of all the centroids and picked the axis with the largest extent. I used the midpoint of that centroid box along that axis as the split point. I then partitioned the primitives by whether their centroid was below the split point. If everything ended up on one side, I just split the range in half so the recursion doesn't get stuck.

For bounding box intersection, I used the slab method. For each axis I computed the entering and exiting t, swapped them if needed when the ray direction was negative, and kept taking the intersection of the intervals. If the interval became empty the ray missed the box.

For BVH traversal, I first tested the bounding box and returned false immediately if the ray missed. For leaf nodes I tested all primitives directly. For has_intersection I returned true on the first hit. For intersect I checked both children and let the primitive intersection functions update max_t, so farther hits get ignored automatically without me having to track them separately.

I compared rendering times with and without BVH. Banana went from 1.9 sec to 0.12 sec. Cow went from 10.96 sec to 0.21 sec. Maxplanck was the biggest difference, from 226 sec down to 0.26 sec. Part 3: Direct Illumination

For the diffuse BSDF, I implemented Lambertian reflection. The function f(wo, wi) just returns reflectance / PI. For sample_f, I used the cosine-weighted hemisphere sampler to pick a direction, stored it in wi, set the pdf, and returned f.

For zero-bounce illumination, I returned the emission of the hit surface directly. So if a camera ray hits a light, the pixel gets the light's emission. This made the ceiling light show up in the Cornell box.

For uniform hemisphere sampling, I sampled random directions on the hemisphere above the hit point, converted to world space, and cast shadow rays. If the ray hit something emissive, I added the contribution using the BSDF, cosine term, and hemisphere pdf, then averaged over all samples. It works but it's noisy because most random directions don't hit the light.

For importance sampling, I sampled directions toward the light sources directly. For each sample I cast a shadow ray toward the light and if nothing blocked it, I added the contribution using the BSDF, cosine term, emitted radiance, and the light's pdf. This gives much cleaner results since the samples are actually aimed at the lights.

Part 4: Global Illumination

For indirect lighting, the main change was in at_least_one_bounce_radiance. After computing direct lighting at the hit point, I sampled a new direction from the BSDF using sample_f, converted it to world space, and cast a new ray from the hit point. If it hit something, I recursively called at_least_one_bounce_radiance on the new intersection and scaled the result by the BSDF value and cosine term, then divided by the pdf. The new ray gets depth r.depth - 1 so the recursion terminates at max_ray_depth.

I also updated raytrace_pixel so camera rays start with depth = max_ray_depth, and updated est_radiance_global_illumination to return zero-bounce plus the recursive bounce term.

For Russian Roulette, I always kept at least one indirect bounce when indirect lighting was on, then used a continuation probability for later bounces. If the path got terminated I just stopped, otherwise I divided by the continuation probability to keep the estimate unbiased.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Adaptive sampling lets you stop sampling a pixel early once it has converged, so you don't waste samples on regions that are already clean.

I implemented this in raytrace_pixel. While sampling a pixel I kept a running sum of illuminance values and a running sum of squared illuminance values. Every samplesPerBatch samples I computed the mean and variance from those sums and checked the convergence condition. The condition is \( I = 1.96 \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \leq \text{maxTolerance} \cdot \mu \). If it passed, I stopped early. I stored the actual sample count used in sampleCountBuffer so the sampling rate image could be generated.

In the sampling rate images, flat and simple regions converge early and use fewer samples. Edges, shadow boundaries, and noisier areas use more. The final image still looks clean, but the sample count is not wasted on the easy parts.
